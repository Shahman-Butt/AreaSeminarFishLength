PART A — THE TASK IN ONE PAGE
================================================================================

PROBLEM
  Fish on commercial boats and in research surveys are measured by hand,
  which is slow, repetitive, and expensive. Length data matters for stock
  assessment, legal minimum sizes, and fisheries management. Automating the
  measurement using a camera + AI would scale much better.

WHAT THE SEMINAR ASKS (verbatim, decoded into 3 sub-questions)
  "How well does an established deep learning baseline perform for fish
   length estimation, and does replacing its encoder with a Vision
   Foundation Model (VFM) improve performance, in particular when labeled
   data are limited?"

  Q1  Reproduce the published baseline on the AutoFish dataset.
  Q2  Replace the encoder (MobileNetV2) with a VFM (e.g. DINOv2). Compare.
  Q3  Repeat the comparison under reduced training data. THIS IS THE
      HEADLINE EXPERIMENT — the "in particular" clause is the heart of it.

OPTIONAL EXTENSION
  Test transfer to a second fish dataset (the North Sea dataset) if it
  becomes available during the project.

DATASET — AutoFish (Bengtson et al., WACVW 2025)
  https://huggingface.co/datasets/vapaau/autofish

PRIMARY METRIC
  Mean Absolute Error (MAE) in cm, reported separately on:
    - non-occluded fish (Set1 + Set2)
    - occluded fish (All)

BAR TO BEAT
  Published baseline: MAE 0.62 cm (no-occlusion) / 1.38 cm (occluded)

PART B — KEY CONCEPTS, IN PLAIN LANGUAGE
================================================================================

REGRESSION vs CLASSIFICATION
  Classification picks a category. Regression outputs a continuous number.
  This project is regression — predicting length in cm.

ENCODER + HEAD
  Every modern vision network has two parts:
    Encoder (backbone) — big, turns image into a summary of numbers.
    Head — small, turns the summary into the final answer.
  This project swaps ONLY the encoder.

PRETRAINING / TRANSFER LEARNING
  Networks are first trained on large general datasets (e.g. ImageNet),
  then fine-tuned to the specific task. Much better than training from
  scratch when our own data is small.

FINE-TUNING vs FREEZING
  Fine-tuning: continue training the pretrained encoder on our data.
  Freezing: keep encoder fixed, only train the small head. Good when
  labels are few.

VISION FOUNDATION MODELS (VFMs)
  Newer, larger pretrained encoders trained self-supervised on hundreds of
  millions of unlabelled images.
    DINOv2 / DINOv3 (Meta)   — current default first choice
    SAM / SAM 2 image enc.   — shape-aware, good for measurement
    CLIP                     — more semantic, less geometric
  Claim: features so rich that you often get good results with a frozen
  encoder and a tiny head. Should especially help when labels are scarce.

SELF-SUPERVISED LEARNING
  Train on images alone, no human labels. Typical trick: mask part of an
  image and ask the model to fill it in. The model accidentally learns
  rich features.

INSTANCE SEGMENTATION MASK
  A pixel-precise outline of one object in an image. AutoFish provides
  one for every fish.

OCCLUSION
  When one fish partially covers another. AutoFish's "All" subsets are
  deliberately full of occlusion.

GROUP-LEVEL SPLIT (most important practical concept)
  Each individual fish in AutoFish appears in exactly ONE of 25 groups.
  Splitting by group ensures no fish leaks across train/val/test.
  Splitting by image would let the model memorize individual fish.

PART C — PAPER 1: AutoFish (Bengtson et al., WACVW 2025)
================================================================================

WHAT IT IS
  A new public fish-image dataset + two example methods (segmentation +
  length estimation). arXiv:2501.03767.

DATASET FACTS
  Images           1,500 RGB, 2464 × 2056 px
  Unique fish      454
  Instance masks   18,160
  Groups           25 (14–24 fish per group)
  Species          cod, haddock, whiting, hake, horse mackerel
  Length labels    measured by marine biologist, rounded to nearest 5 mm
  Camera           Jai GO-5100C-USB + KOWA LM12HC lens, 1.5 m above belt
  Calibration      20 checkerboard images per group (20 mm squares)

THREE SUBSETS PER GROUP (gives 60 images × 25 groups = 1,500)
  Set1   half the fish, no overlap, 20 photos.
  Set2   other half, no overlap, 20 photos.
  All    every fish in group, deliberately overlapping, 20 photos.

THE GROUP STRUCTURE — KEY POINT
  Each fish only ever appears in one group. Splitting by group prevents
  leakage. Splitting by image creates leakage. Mention this in the meeting.

BASELINE 1 — SKL (no learning)
  Skeletonize the mask, fit a 4th-degree polynomial along the centerline,
  measure length in pixels, convert to cm via calibration. Classical
  image processing.

BASELINE 2 — REG (THE one I have to reproduce)
  Crop fish from bounding box of mask, blackout outside.
  → MobileNetV2 encoder (ImageNet-pretrained)
  → FC head (2 layers) + bounding-box coordinates as extra input
  → output: length in cm
  Training: L1 loss, Adam, LR 0.001, batch 32, 200 epochs.
  Augmentation: only contrast/brightness/saturation (no geometric — would
  break the implicit pixel-to-cm relationship).

REPORTED RESULTS
  REG on Set1/Set2 (no-occlusion):   MAE 0.62 cm
  REG on All (occluded):             MAE 1.38 cm
  These are the numbers my reproduction must approach.

PART D — PAPER 2: Herrmann et al. (2024)
================================================================================

WHAT IT IS
  "Can automatic measuring replace humans when evaluating a shrimp
   fishery?" Regional Studies in Marine Science, vol. 79, art. 103852.
  Not a CV-benchmark paper — a fisheries-science paper asking a
  practical management question.

QUESTION
  Can a camera + AI replace manual calliper measurements of shrimp
  carapace length, while still giving valid scientific conclusions for
  stock assessment, gear-selectivity studies, and compliance monitoring?

EXPERIMENT
  Real research cruise (R/V Helmer Hanssen, Barents Sea, Feb 2023).
  2,167 deep-water shrimp (Pandalus borealis), each measured BOTH
  manually (calliper) and automatically (camera).
  Camera: off-the-shelf Intel RealSense D405 (RGB + depth).

CLEVER METHOD
  Don't measure length directly — too sensitive to segmentation errors
  at the carapace endpoints. Instead:
    1. AI segments the shrimp.
    2. Compute 3-D volume from RGB + depth.
    3. Convert volume → carapace length using a known correlation.
  Why volume? Boundary pixels contribute little to volume — much more
  robust to segmentation noise than measuring length directly.

ACCURACY NUMBERS (manual vs auto on individual shrimp)
  Mean absolute deviation:  0.55 mm
  Mean signed deviation:    ~0 mm   (essentially unbiased)
  Std dev of per-shrimp error: 0.72 mm
  Most errors within ±2.5 mm or ±10%.

THE BIG METHODOLOGICAL POINT — "use-case-driven evaluation"
  Don't judge a measurement system by per-individual error alone.
  Re-run the actual downstream analyses with both datasets and check
  if the SCIENTIFIC CONCLUSIONS change. They demonstrate that for all
  three use cases (stock assessment, gear selectivity, compliance) the
  conclusions are the same — so the automated method is "good enough"
  even though individual measurements have small errors.

TAKE-AWAYS FOR MY PROJECT
  1. Real-world motivation for the seminar's topic — perfect framing.
  2. MAE alone is not the whole story. In my discussion I should reflect
     on whether residual error would change downstream conclusions, not
     just report numbers.
  3. RGB-D / depth and proxy quantities (volume, area) are interesting
     forward-looking ideas if anyone asks "what else could you try?"

PART J — POTENTIAL DIFFICULT QUESTIONS + MY ANSWERS
================================================================================

Q: Why DINOv2 and not SAM or CLIP?
A: DINOv2 transfers well to fine-grained / dense tasks without fine-tuning,
   which matches the "limited labels" question. SAM is also reasonable —
   I'd compare to it if time allows. CLIP is more semantic, less geometric,
   so a worse first choice for measurement.

Q: How do you know your baseline reproduction is faithful?
A: Same architecture, same loss family (L1/Smooth-L1), same metric (MAE),
   same two evaluation regimes (no-occlusion / occluded). I report my
   number next to the paper's 0.62 / 1.38. Within ~0.2 cm = credible.

Q: What if DINOv2 doesn't beat MobileNetV2 at full data?
A: Valid scientific outcome — I'd report it honestly. The interesting
   experiment is the small-data regime. Even ties at 100% but wins at 10%
   is the result the task is asking about.

Q: Why crops, not whole images?
A: Whole-image regression conflates "which fish?" with "how long?".
   Cropping isolates the regression problem; matches the paper.

Q: Why not also replace segmentation with a VFM?
A: Task asks to swap the encoder of the length-estimation baseline.
   Segmentation is a separate problem; conflating both muddies the
   comparison. I use GT masks at evaluation, like the paper.

Q: Is 1,500 images enough?
A: It's tight — that's exactly why this is an interesting problem and why
   VFMs may help. The data-efficiency experiment makes this an explicit
   variable rather than a hidden confounder.

Q: Are the labels precise enough?
A: Lengths rounded to 5 mm → MAE floor of ~0.25 cm. Won't chase below that.
